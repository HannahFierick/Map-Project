LinkNYC remains controversial in some NYC neighborhoods

Free Wifi structures across the city are causing both an increase in connectivity and complaints

By Hannah Fierick

12/19/2023

Photo via: Medium

LinkNYC, a program founded by CityBridge in 2015 to bring free wifi to New Yorkers, has played a crucial role in accessible connectivity. However, since their founding, they have been accused of failing to live up to their promises about serving low-income communities, and the majority of their stations remain in Manhattan.

In 2021, they pledged that Ninety percent of their new structures would be installed in the outer boroughs and above 96th Street in Manhattan to expand connectivity to underserved communities.

The controversey has also come from manhattan residents and local preservationists and the New York State Historic Preservation Office. Complaints say that the 32-foot tall LinkNYC 5G towers will obstruct intricate details on some of the historic buildings located in the West Village and the Upper East Side Neighborhoods.

LinkNYC eventually hopes to have upwards of 2,500 stations in Manhattan. Currently, there are 2,083 active stations across all 5 boroughs.

“We deserve the best design possible for our streets, and to re-utilize the existing infrastructure, and that’s a big word that means street lights, newsstands, power pits, so it does not unduly affect the effects of a historic streetscape while still increasing broadband access,” preservation consultant Simeon Bankoff told NY1 in November.
